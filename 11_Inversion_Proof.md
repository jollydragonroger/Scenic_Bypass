## 11.3 The Incompleteness of Physics Alone

### 11.3.1 The Measurement Problem (Revisited)

Quantum mechanics describes:
1. Unitary evolution: |Ψ(t)⟩ = U(t)|Ψ(0)⟩ (deterministic, reversible)
2. Measurement: |Ψ⟩ → |ψᵢ⟩ with probability |αᵢ|² (probabilistic, irreversible)

**The Problem:** These two processes are incompatible:
- Unitary evolution is linear; measurement is nonlinear
- Unitary evolution is reversible; measurement is irreversible
- Unitary evolution applies always—except when measurement happens

No purely physical mechanism explains the transition.

### 11.3.2 The Origin Problem

Physics describes how systems evolve but not why they exist:
- Why are the physical constants what they are?
- Why is there something rather than nothing?
- Why these laws and not others?

These are not scientific failures—they are **boundary conditions** that physics cannot address from within.

### 11.3.3 The Meaning Problem

Physics describes correlations but not significance:
- The wavelength of light is 550 nm (physical fact)
- That wavelength is "green" (perceptual fact)
- Green means "go" at traffic lights (cultural fact)
- This green light saved your life (personal significance)

Physics captures the first; it cannot capture the others.

### 11.3.4 Formal Statement of Incompleteness

**Theorem 11.1: Physics is Incomplete**

**Statement:** The physical description of reality, using only physical variables, cannot account for:
- The mechanism of wave function collapse
- The origin of physical constants and laws
- The significance of physical facts

**Proof (Sketch):**
```
Let P = set of all physical propositions
Let S = set of all true propositions about reality

The Measurement Problem shows: ∃ propositions about collapse ∉ P
The Origin Problem shows: ∃ propositions about existence ∉ P
The Meaning Problem shows: ∃ propositions about significance ∉ P

Physics is incomplete: there are truths it cannot express.
∎
```
